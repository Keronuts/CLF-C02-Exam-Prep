Let's look at our first of three service types at Amazon Web Services, Infrastructure as a Service or IaaS. IaaS offers the basic building blocks for cloud information technology. It provides access to networking features, computers (virtual or on dedicated hardware), and data storage space in the AWS datacenter. Infrastructure as a Service delivers customers the highest level of flexibility and management control over their IT resources. This type most closely compares to existing IT resources that many IT departments and developers are familiar with today. At AWS, there is a clear demarcation point between the provider's responsibility below the dash line and the customer responsibility above the dash line. AWS provides the data centers in Availability Zones in regions all over the world.

They also have access to edge locations to customers. That's the Global Infrastructure. They provide their own identity and access management, specifically a zero-trust environment in their data centers. They provide secure endpoints for customers to use to get access to a myriad of services at AWS. They also provide foundation services, compute, storage, database, networking and content distribution networking, including a fifth foundation service, most likely machine learning and AI. Everything above that is the customer's decision responsibility. How data is encrypted, the operating system to deploy, the network configuration, the platform and application management, firewall configuration. The customer decides about identity and access management, and of course, customer data.

Platform as a Service removes the need for organizations to manage the underlying infrastructure, the hardware and operating systems. But it also lets customers focus on the deployment and management of their applications often at the cloud provider. PaaS allows customers to relax and not be so concerned with resource procurement, capacity planning, software maintenance, patching, or any of the other undifferentiated heavy lifting involved in running applications or databases.

AWS Platform as a Service includes things like development and software development kit platforms for Java, PHP, Python, and more. Container services for Docker and Kubernetes orchestration. Managed and fully managed relational and document databases, not to mention graph databases and blockchain databases. Managed security and threat modeling services and single sign-on, machine learning, AI, IoT, blockchain, and other media services. These all fall under the category of Platform as a Service. And remember, the demarcation between the customer responsibility and AWS's responsibility can differ even within a database portfolio. Some are managed, some are fully managed. Software as a Service offers customers a comprehensive solution run and managed almost entirely by the service provider. In most cases, this involves various end-user applications. With a SaaS offering, customers are not involved with the maintenance of the service or the underlying infrastructure.

A common example of a SaaS application is a web-based email or web-based personal cloud storage. Common SaaS offerings include customer relationship management (CRM), enterprise resource management (ERM), human resources and workplace tools, finance, sales, and marketing services, payroll services, cloud-based email, collaboration, and cloud storage, virtual help desk and virtual service desk, virtual call center, and business analytics. On the exam, remember the three types Infrastructure, Platform, and Software as a Service. And also remember, as far as the shared responsibility model goes, the customer has the most responsibility with Infrastructure as a Service and the least responsibility with Software as a Service.

And with Platform as a Service, there can be a lot of variance and differentiation depending upon the service and the portfolio offering within the service. In addition to three service types, there's also three deployment models at Amazon Web Services. The first one is the cloud deployment model. A cloud-based application is fully deployed in the cloud and all parts of the application or database solution run in the cloud. Applications in the cloud have either been generated in the cloud or have been migrated from a standing infrastructure to leverage the benefits of AWS cloud computing. Cloud-based applications, for example, containerized applications can be constructed on low-level infrastructure pieces or they can use higher-level services that offer abstraction from the management, architecting, and scaling needs of the core infrastructure.

With this model, whatever the program or the initiative, it all is migrated or running in the cloud. The second model, according to AWS is the hybrid cloud deployment. This model is a method for connecting infrastructure and applications between cloud-based resources and existing resources that are not placed in the cloud. In other words, on-premises. The most common method is between the cloud and existing on-premises infrastructure to extend and grow an organization's infrastructure into the cloud while connecting cloud resources to internal systems. Online retailers may use hybrid cloud for "bursting up". For example, during peak seasons, the holidays in the United States, or an online travel agent whose peak season is made through September deprovisioning at the end of the peak season.

The third model is the on-premises model. Installing resources on-premises, using virtualization and resource management tools, is often called a private cloud. On-premises deployment does not provide many of the benefits of cloud computing but is often chosen for its ability to provide dedicated resources. In most scenarios, this deployment model is the same as legacy IT infrastructure while using application management and virtualization technologies to try and increase resource utilization or lower a carbon footprint, or move to a smaller, more compact data center. On the exam, remember that the on-premises cloud deployment model does not use Amazon Web Services.

  • outline how to achieve high availability by using multiple Availability Zones
[Video description begins] Topic title: Multiple Availability Zones. Presented by: Michael Shannon. [Video description ends]

Using multiple Availability Zones, every AWS region is segmented into distinct Availability Zones, or AZs. Each AZ has its own power, cooling, and network connectivity forming an isolated failure domain. Within the AWS domain, customers are encouraged to run their workloads in more than one Availability Zone. This ensures that customer applications can survive even a complete AZ failure, which is still a very rare event at Amazon Web Services. On the exam, you may need to know about AWS placement groups. A placement group is a configuration option that AWS offers that lets customers house a group of interdependent EC2 instances in a certain way across the underlying hardware on which those instances reside.

The instances could be placed close together or spread through different racks, or even spread through different Availability Zones. Objects in a Simple Storage Service (S3) Standard tier are automatically replicated by default across three availability zones in a region. In an Amazon Relational Database (RDS) Multi-AZ deployment, Amazon RDS automatically creates a primary database instance and synchronously replicates the data to an instance in a different AZ in the same region. When a failure occurs and is detected, RDS automatically fails over to a standby instance without any manual intervention. Remember, every region in the AWS Global Infrastructure has at least 3 Availability Zones, some have 4, even up to six. In the next lesson, we'll go beyond just using multiple Availability Zones, and we'll discuss using multiple regions.

8. Video: Benefits of Edge Locations (it_clawsp23_08_enus_08)

Upon completion of this video, you will be able to recognize the benefits of edge locations, e.g., Amazon CloudFront, AWS Global Accelerator.

  • recognize the benefits of edge locations, e.g., Amazon CloudFront, AWS Global Accelerator
[Video description begins] Topic title: Benefits of Edge Locations. Presented by: Michael Shannon. [Video description ends]

Edge Locations. Edge locations or AWS data centers, and metropolitan area partners that are designed to deliver customer services with the lowest possible latency. Amazon has dozens of these data centers spread across the world. They are closer to users than regions or even availability zones. They're often in major cities, so responses to customers and streaming users can be extremely fast and low latency. The benefits of edge locations are that Amazon CloudFront content distribution or delivery networking, uses edge locations. It caches copies of the content that it serves, presenting it closer to consumers and delivering it to them faster. Route 53 serves DNS responses from edge locations so that DNS queries that originate nearby can resolve faster. Web application firewall (WAF) and AWS Shield (Standard and Advanced) filter traffic in edge locations to stop unwanted traffic as soon as possible, denial-of-service (DDoS), and bot traffic.

AWS Global Accelerator lets customers route requests for key resources through Amazon's global network, even if the request is going halfway around the world. The request is originally routed to the closest edge location using Anycasting, and then travels through Amazon's network with lower latency and higher throughput than the public Internet. Here we can see a simple diagram showing edge locations. Viewers get their content, streaming video, streaming audio, other types of data from edge locations serviced through Amazon CloudFront. Typically, the edge locations use Elastic Cache with Redis, in other words, in-memory storage as opposed to solid-state drive arrays for fast delivery and low latency. Edge locations are actually serviced by regional edge caches. The origin server is the original content and it can be stored in AWS S3 buckets, which is the most common solution.
